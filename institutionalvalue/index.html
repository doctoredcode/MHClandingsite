What does it mean for us when the most expensive care we render at the bedside is most often the care our patients wanted least?

The U.S. population over 65 is set to nearly double by 2050, with a growing share of terminally ill patients committed to intensive care few would actually choose. Early goals-of-care discussions can realign that care with patients' values, lower the cost of unwanted treatment, and improve hospital metrics, yet they remain widely underused and unevenly implemented. We're here to change that.

The Problem

The most expensive care is often the care no one asked for.

Seriously ill patients account for a growing share of hospital admissions, ICU days, and 30-day readmissions, much of it due to aggressive care that was never aligned with their values, because no one asked what mattered most to them. When surveyed, more than 80% of Americans say they would prefer less aggressive care at the end of life.

Where The Obvious Fixes Fall Short

Education, culture change, and expanded palliative coverage have failed to scale, study after study.

It starts with two barriers every study points to, neither of which education or culture change can fix.

# 1 Studied Barrier
Time
Across national surveys, physicians rank time as the number one barrier to goals-of-care discussions, above discomfort or fear of taking away hope. A quality conversation takes 45 to 60 minutes of uninterrupted bedside time that an acute-care shift rarely allows.
Skill: The Training Gap
< 30%
Fewer than 30% of physicians have any formal training in goals-of-care discussions, even though nearly all agree it matters. Most feel unprepared, and self-rated confidence rarely matches the quality patients actually experience.

Across landmark trials and real-world rollouts, each of these falls short.

Understanding Your Cost Exposure

How well do you really know what runs under your own hood?

Data is power. Four questions worth asking within your own institution.

1
What share of your seriously ill patients receive a goals-of-care discussion early in their hospital course?
That one number reveals your exposure to the cost and resource drain of value-discordant care.
2
Does your practice culture defer every goals-of-care discussion to palliative consultants, or only the complex ones?
Routing simple goals-of-care discussions to palliative consultants is like consulting cardiology to read a basic EKG: costly and resource-intensive. Let MHC handle the routine cases, and reserve your consultants for the complex ones.
3
Are you measuring the quality of those conversations, or only that they happened?
Quality discussions rely on the structured, evidence-based frameworks palliative physicians train on. MHC is built on those same frameworks, so any physician can lead one with the same rigor.
4
What is value-discordant care costing you each month, in beds, ICU days, and readmission penalties?
A hospital bed is a finite resource. Reserving each bed, ICU day, and aggressive treatment for the patients who want and consent to it is also how you meet Age-Friendly Health System measures.
